## Hook Patterns

The hook (first 1-2 lines visible before "see more") determines ~80% of a post's performance.

### 1. Bold Claim / Contrarian Take
```
Most companies get [X] completely wrong.
Here's what actually works:
```

### 2. Specific Number / Metric
```
We printed and shipped 8,000 pairs of custom sweatpants in two months.
And saved $70K doing it.
```

### 3. Story Opener
```
Last week a customer told me something that changed how I think about [X].
```

### 4. Pattern Interrupt
```
WARNING: this is a long post.
But if you're running a merch program at scale, it might save you serious headaches.
```

### 5. Confession / Vulnerability
```
We spent nine months on an SEO strategy, and it failed.
Here's what we learned:
```

### 6. List Promise
```
5 things I wish I knew before scaling our branded merchandise program.
(#3 is the one most companies skip)
```

### 7. Question Hook
```
Why does ordering 500 branded hoodies still require 47 emails?
The answer is simpler than you think.
```

### 8. Build-in-Public Update
```
Quarterly update from the trenches:
Revenue: up X%
Clients: X new logos
Biggest lesson this quarter:
```

### 9. "Here's the playbook" (Educational)
```
We helped [Company] launch their employee onboarding kit in 2 weeks.
Here's exactly how we did it:
```

### 10. Industry Observation
```
Something interesting is happening in corporate merch right now.
The companies doing it well have one thing in common:
```

## Post Formats

### Text-Only
Best for: hot takes, personal stories, lessons learned, founder reflections.
Sweet spot: 200-400 words (1,200-2,500 characters). Posts in this range perform ~32% better.
```
[Hook — 1-2 lines]

[Line break]

[Body — story, framework, or argument]
[Short paragraphs, 1-3 sentences each]
[Whitespace between paragraphs]

[Closing — takeaway, question, or soft CTA]
```

### Text + Image
Best for: product shots, data/charts, before/after, behind-the-scenes photos, brand campaign examples.
The image must add proof or context — no generic stock photos.

### Carousel (PDF Document)
Best for: listicles, step-by-step guides, frameworks, brand campaign breakdowns.
- 6-12 slides sweet spot
- First slide = hook (treat it like a post hook)
- One idea per slide, large text, minimal design
- Last slide = CTA or summary

### Text + Video
Best for: product demos, warehouse/ops behind-the-scenes, founder talks.
Keep under 90 seconds for feed. Captions mandatory.

### Poll
Best for: engagement spikes, audience research. Use sparingly (max 1/week).

## Founder vs Company Page

### Founder Account (Primary Distribution)
- 5-10x organic reach vs company pages
- Best for: opinions, stories, lessons, build-in-public, hiring, industry takes
- Voice: personal, specific, opinionated

### Company Page (Supporting)
- Best for: product announcements, customer spotlights, team culture, industry tips, job posts
- Voice: warm and witty (like beehiiv), not corporate press releases
- Reshare/amplify founder posts; post own content with personality

### Synergy
1. Founder posts story → company page reshares with added context
2. Company page posts case study → founder shares with personal commentary
3. Founder tags company → company engages in comments

## ICP Context

Content should resonate with these audiences:

**Primary: Marketing, Sales, HR, and Ops leaders** at:
- Tech companies and professional services firms
- Companies with 100+ employees OR $100M+ annual revenue
- Recently funded, fast-growing companies

**What they care about:**
- Employer brand and employee experience
- Event marketing and brand activations
- Client gifting and sales enablement
- Operational efficiency (fewer vendors, less manual work)
- Making their programs look impressive with less effort
- Budget justification and measurable ROI

**Where they feel pain:**
- Managing multiple merch vendors is chaotic
- Quality control is unpredictable
- Reordering is manual and slow
- No visibility into inventory or spend
- Merch programs feel like an afterthought — disorganized, last-minute

## Content Types That Drive Awareness and Interest

Ranked by effectiveness for the ICP:

### 1. Operational Transparency / Behind-the-Scenes (High engagement)
Show the messy reality of merch ops. Warehouse tours, production timelines, vendor management, quality checks. People love seeing how things actually work.

### 2. Brand Campaign Breakdowns (High shareability)
Analyze real campaigns — why a company's onboarding kit worked, what made a conference activation memorable, how a startup used merch to build culture. Name the brand, show the merch, explain the strategy. (Get permission or use public examples.)

### 3. Failure Stories / Lessons Learned (High trust-building)
Share what went wrong and what you learned. Everest's SEO post is a template — honest, specific, useful. These build credibility faster than wins.

### 4. Data and Insights (High save/bookmark rate)
Industry benchmarks, cost breakdowns, trend analysis. "The average company wastes $X on merch they never distribute." Data stops the scroll.

### 5. Customer Transformation Stories (High conversion intent)
Before/after narratives. "Company X was managing merch across 4 vendors with spreadsheets. Now they [result]." The customer is the hero; Stitchi is the enabler.

### 6. "Secret" Tips and Vendor Knowledge (High value perception)
Insider knowledge about sourcing, materials, printing techniques, cost-saving hacks. Things only someone deep in the industry would know. This positions the author as the expert.

### 7. Hot Takes on Industry Trends (High comment volume)
"Corporate swag is dying. Here's what's replacing it." Provocative but backed by real observation. Drives comments and debate.

### 8. Team and Culture Posts (Warm engagement, hiring magnet)
New hires, team milestones, company culture moments. Less reach than educational content, but builds the human side.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Corporate jargon.** "Excited to announce our innovative solution" → nobody reads past line 1
2. **Only BOFU content.** Constant product pitches = unfollowed
3. **Obviously AI-written.** If it sounds like ChatGPT default voice, people scroll past
4. **No engagement.** Post then ghost. Must reply to comments within 1-2 hours
5. **Generic motivation.** "Success is a journey" adds zero value
6. **Hashtag stuffing.** 0-3 max, only if genuinely relevant
7. **Inconsistency.** 5 posts one week, gone for a month — destroys momentum
8. **Stock photos.** Use real product shots, real team photos, real screenshots
9. **Link-in-first-comment gimmick.** Tired. Put links where they naturally fit.
10. **Writing for other founders instead of the ICP.** The audience is marketing/HR/ops leaders, not other startup founders (unless it's a build-in-public post, which serves a different purpose)
