# Competitive Positioning & Objection Handling

## Competitive Landscape

Stitchi competes against three categories:

### 1. Large Promo Distributors (4imprint, HALO, Staples Promotional)
**Their pitch:** Scale, catalog breadth, low unit cost.
**Where they win:** Commoditized, high-volume, price-sensitive buyers.
**Where Stitchi wins:** They're order-takers, not program builders. No dedicated AM, no store technology, no strategic guidance. Client does all the work.
**Counter-position:** "We don't just fulfill orders — we run your program. One point of contact, a branded storefront, and a team that proactively manages your catalog, not a 1-800 number."

### 2. Store Platform Vendors (OrderMyGear, Printful, Swag.com, SwagUp)
**Their pitch:** Self-serve technology, low startup cost, fast launch.
**Where they win:** Small teams, DIY buyers, simple use cases.
**Where Stitchi wins:** They sell software; you still need to run the program. No account management, limited decoration options, no warehousing/kitting, no strategic curation.
**Counter-position:** "Technology is one piece — we combine the platform with dedicated service, creative curation, and logistics so you don't need internal resources to run a world-class program."

### 3. Local Promo Shops / Print Shops
**Their pitch:** Relationship, proximity, flexibility.
**Where they win:** Small orders, tight timelines, existing relationships.
**Where Stitchi wins:** They can't scale. No online store, no inventory management, no data/reporting, no brand consistency controls.
**Counter-position:** "We're local too — three Michigan offices — but with the technology and supplier network to scale your program nationally without losing the personal touch."

## Common Objections & Proposal Language

### "You're too small / too new"
**Reality:** Founded 2021, 175K+ items fulfilled, 500+ suppliers, serving recognizable brands.
**In proposal:** Don't be defensive. Lead with proof points and named clients. The case studies section and partner logos carry more weight than years-in-business claims. Frame youth as agility: "We were purpose-built for modern merch programs — no legacy systems, no outdated processes."

### "We already have a vendor"
**In proposal:** Don't trash the incumbent. Acknowledge what's working, then frame Stitchi as an evolution: "Many of our clients previously worked with traditional distributors. What they gained with Stitchi was visibility, consistency, and time back — the same quality products, now managed through a modern platform with dedicated support."

### "We can do this in-house"
**In proposal:** Quantify the hidden cost: internal coordination time, vendor management, inventory tracking, employee support tickets. "Organizations with 500+ employees typically spend 15–20 hours/month managing merch internally. Our program gives you that time back while improving the employee experience."

### "Your pricing seems high"
**In proposal:** Reframe from unit cost to total cost of ownership. Store hosting, AM support, fulfillment, returns — these are included, not hidden. "Our transparent pricing means no surprise fees. The store hosting, dedicated account management, and employee support that would cost $XX/month elsewhere are built into our program."

### "Can you match [competitor]'s pricing?"
**In proposal:** Never compete on price alone. Shift to value: "We'd rather show you why our clients stay — 95%+ retention — than win on price. The real cost difference is measured in hours saved, brand consistency gained, and employee satisfaction improved."
